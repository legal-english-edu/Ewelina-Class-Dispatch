dispatch/dɪˈspætʃ/
noun

2. The act of sending someone or something promptly to a particular place.

Lesson 1 – Vocabulary
🟠  Communication Basics
I hope everything will be OK
Mam nadzieję, że wszystko będzie dobrze
I hope everything will be OK after the meeting tomorrow.
Mam nadzieję, że po jutrzejszym spotkaniu wszystko będzie dobrze.
✅ I hope everything will be OK. – natural structure.
🚫 I hope so everything… – so cannot be placed there.
I think so / I don’t think so
Tak mi się wydaje / Chyba nie
I think so, but I need to check the details first.
Tak mi się wydaje, ale najpierw muszę sprawdzić szczegóły.
✅ I don’t think so. – natural in everyday conversation.
🚫 I think not. – possible, but formal and old-fashioned.
I have a question
Mam pytanie
I have a question about the new procedure.
Mam pytanie dotyczące nowej procedury.
when it comes to speaking
jeśli chodzi o mówienie
When it comes to speaking, I need more confidence.
Jeśli chodzi o mówienie, potrzebuję więcej pewności siebie.
I would prefer something more professional
Wolałabym coś bardziej profesjonalnego
For this meeting, I would prefer something more professional.
Na to spotkanie wolałabym coś bardziej profesjonalnego.
🔵  Work & Career
at work / in my work
w pracy / w mojej pracy
I do not use much English at work.
Nie używam zbyt wiele angielskiego w pracy.
English is very important in my work.
Angielski jest bardzo ważny w mojej pracy.
✅ at work – the workplace as a place or general context.
✅ in my work – the work you actually do, your professional activity.
🚫 in my work at work – redundant; never combine both.
promotion
awans
She got a promotion after two years in the company.
Po dwóch latach w firmie dostała awans.
to get promoted
dostać awans
When I think about getting promoted, English is absolutely necessary in my company.
Kiedy myślę o awansie, angielski jest absolutnie niezbędny w mojej firmie.
Key Opinion Leaders
kluczowi liderzy opinii
Many companies work with Key Opinion Leaders in the medical sector.
Wiele firm współpracuje z kluczowymi liderami opinii w sektorze medycznym.
to wait for another meeting
poczekać na kolejne spotkanie
We need to wait for another meeting before we make a final decision.
Musimy poczekać na kolejne spotkanie, zanim podejmiemy ostateczną decyzję.
🟢  Medical English
haemophilia /ˌhiːməˈfɪliə/
hemofilia
Her son was diagnosed with haemophilia when he was very young.
U jej syna zdiagnozowano hemofilię, gdy był bardzo mały.
✅ haemophilia – British English spelling.
✅ hemophilia – American English spelling.
Both are correct. Do not treat one as the only right form.
medications
leki
The patient takes several medications every day.
Pacjent codziennie przyjmuje kilka leków.
✅ medications – the right word in clinical and professional contexts.
🚫 drugs – ambiguous; can mean illegal narcotics. Avoid in medical settings.
this product is called…
ten produkt nazywa się…
This product is called a portable blood warmer.
Ten produkt nazywa się przenośny podgrzewacz krwi.
a native English speaker
native speaker języka angielskiego
My friend, who is a native English speaker, is from the Philippines.
Mój znajomy, który jest native speakerem języka angielskiego, jest z Filipin.
✅ a native English speaker – standard word order.
🚫 an English native speaker – awkward; avoid.
🟡  Quantity & Determiners
a few problems
kilka problemów
We had a few problems, but we solved them quickly.
Mieliśmy kilka problemów, ale szybko je rozwiązaliśmy.
few problems
niewiele problemów
We had few problems during the project.
Podczas projektu mieliśmy niewiele problemów.
✅ a few problems – means some problems. Neutral or positive.
✅ few problems – means not many, closer to none.
🚫 Do not use them interchangeably. The meaning changes.
another / other / the other
inny / inne / ten drugi
Another colleague asked the same question. Other people may disagree. The other option is better.
Inny kolega zadał to samo pytanie. Inni ludzie mogą się nie zgadzać. Ta druga opcja jest lepsza.
✅ another – singular only. Means inny or jeszcze jeden: another meeting.
✅ other – plural or uncountable: other people, other options.
✅ the other – the remaining one in a specific, defined group.
🚫 Do not use them interchangeably.
One Thing From Today
Another, other, and the other look similar but work differently. Another is always singular – one more, or a different one. Other is used with plurals and uncountable nouns. The other points to what remains in a specific, defined group. Getting these right is a small change with a noticeable effect on precision.

The Meeting Before the Meeting

Anna works in medical sales. English is very important in her work – she uses it almost every day, especially when she prepares for international meetings.

This week, she has a question about a new product her company is launching. The product is called a portable blood warmer. She has read the technical file, but when it comes to speaking about the clinical data in English, she feels less confident than she would like.

She sends a message to her manager: “I have a question about the presentation. Should I focus on the haemophilia indication first, or start with the broader medications data?”

Her manager replies: “I think so – start with haemophilia. It gives the clinical team a clear anchor. But let’s wait for another meeting on Thursday before we confirm the final structure.”

Anna reads the reply twice. I hope everything will be OK, she thinks. I hope the Thursday meeting gives us a clear direction.

She prepares two versions of the opening slide – one more technical, one more accessible. Her colleague suggests a third option: something in between.

“I would prefer something more professional,” Anna says. “The audience includes Key Opinion Leaders. We can’t be too casual.”

Her colleague nods. “I think so too.”

They had a few problems with the data formatting earlier in the week, but they solved them quickly. Now there are few problems left – only the final language review remains.

Anna opens her laptop and starts again.

The Promotion Question

Marek has worked at the same pharmaceutical company for three years. He does not use much English at work – most of his daily communication is in Polish. But in his work, when it comes to reporting to the regional director or presenting to external partners, English is absolutely necessary.

When he thinks about getting promoted, he knows this is the area he needs to develop most. He has talked to another colleague who recently got promoted. That colleague told him the process took time and required strong communication skills – not just technical knowledge.

“Other people in my team got promoted faster,” Marek says to his manager during their quarterly review. “I have a question: what do you think is holding me back?”

His manager pauses. “I think so – communication is part of it. But it’s not only that. There are a few things we need to work on together.”

She lists them: presenting data clearly, handling questions from Key Opinion Leaders, and writing more professionally in English. She also mentions that Marek sometimes confuses another with other – a small thing, but noticeable in written reports.

Marek listens carefully. I hope everything will be OK, he thinks – then stops himself. That is too vague. Instead he asks: “What is the next step? Should I wait for another meeting, or can we start with something practical this week?”

His manager smiles. “The other option – we start now.”

Marek opens his notebook. He has a few questions ready. This time, he is going to ask all of them.
