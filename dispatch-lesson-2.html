Dispatch
dispatch/dɪˈspætʃ/
noun
1. A message or report sent quickly to its destination – especially one sent by a journalist from abroad, or between military officers.
2. The act of sending someone or something promptly to a particular place.

Lesson 2 – Vocabulary
🟠  Apologising for Being Late
Sorry I'm late
Przepraszam, że się spóźniłam / spóźniłem
Sorry I'm late – the traffic was very bad this morning.
Przepraszam, że się spóźniłam – ruch był dziś rano bardzo duży.
Sorry for being late
Przepraszam za spóźnienie
Sorry for being late – my previous meeting finished late.
Przepraszam za spóźnienie – moje poprzednie spotkanie skończyło się późno.
✅ Sorry I'm late – direct, conversational.
✅ Sorry for being late – slightly more formal; common in written messages too.
🔵  Places and Movement
at the car park
na parkingu
I am waiting for you at the car park near the office.
Czekam na ciebie na parkingu obok biura.
through the whole of Warsaw
przez całą Warszawę
We had to drive through the whole of Warsaw to get there.
Musieliśmy przejechać przez całą Warszawę, żeby tam dotrzeć.
✅ through the whole of Warsaw – correct and emphatic.
✅ across Warsaw – often more natural in everyday speech.
🟢  General Phrases
It depends
To zależy
It depends on the client and the situation.
To zależy od klienta i sytuacji.
an issue
sprawa; kwestia; zagadnienie
We need to discuss one issue before the meeting starts.
Musimy omówić jedną kwestię, zanim spotkanie się zacznie.
a question
pytanie
I have a question about the next stage of the project.
Mam pytanie dotyczące kolejnego etapu projektu.
a matter
sprawa; kwestia
This is an important matter for the whole team.
To jest ważna sprawa dla całego zespołu.
Issue, question, and matter can all translate as sprawa or kwestia, but they are not always interchangeable. Matter often signals seriousness. Question implies something to be decided or clarified. Issue often implies a problem or point of discussion.
🟣  Technology
a charger
ładowarka
I need a charger because my battery is almost dead.
Potrzebuję ładowarki, bo bateria jest prawie rozładowana.
to charge the phone
naładować telefon
I need ten minutes to charge the phone before the call.
Potrzebuję dziesięciu minut, żeby naładować telefon przed rozmową.
I can't see the chat box
Nie widzę okna czatu
I can't see the chat box, so please send it again.
Nie widzę okna czatu, więc proszę wyślij to jeszcze raz.
🟡  Work and Courses
to run a course
prowadzić kurs
She runs a course for business professionals.
Ona prowadzi kurs dla profesjonalistów z biznesu.
give an update
przekazać aktualizację; zdać relację z postępów
I usually give an update at the start of the meeting.
Zwykle przekazuję aktualizację na początku spotkania.
update everybody on where you are
poinformować wszystkich, na jakim etapie jesteś
During the call, you update everybody on where you are with the project.
Podczas rozmowy informujesz wszystkich, na jakim etapie jesteś z projektem.
✅ update everybody on where you are – natural spoken business English.
✅ update everybody on your progress – more formal and more standard.
🟤  Emails and Dates
your email of 12 Feb
twój / Pani email z 12 lutego
Thank you for your email of 12 Feb.
Dziękuję za Pani email z 12 lutego.
your email of the twelfth of February
twój / Pani email z dwunastego lutego
I am writing in reply to your email of the twelfth of February.
Piszę w odpowiedzi na Pani email z dwunastego lutego.
your email received on the 12th of February
twój / Pani email otrzymany 12 lutego
I am responding to your email received on the 12th of February.
Odpowiadam na Pani email otrzymany 12 lutego.
All three are correct in formal written English. The received on the… form is particularly common in legal and formal business correspondence.
⚫  Language and Meaning
to contaminate / overload / fill Polish with English borrowings
zanieczyszczać / przeładowywać / wypełniać polszczyznę angielskimi zapożyczeniami
Some speakers worry about contaminating Polish with English borrowings.
Niektórzy użytkownicy języka martwią się o zanieczyszczanie polszczyzny angielskimi zapożyczeniami.
✅ contaminate – correct, but strong in tone.
✅ overload – good if the idea is excess.
✅ fill – less strong, more neutral.
One Thing From Today
Issue, question, and matter all translate as sprawa or kwestia in Polish – but they work differently in English. Matter is serious. Question needs an answer or a decision. Issue points to a problem or a point of discussion. Keeping them separate is a small change with a noticeable effect on precision.

The Long Way Round

Marta joined the call two minutes late. “Sorry I'm late,” she said. “I had to drive through the whole of Warsaw this morning. The traffic was terrible.” Her Swedish colleague smiled. “No problem. We were just starting.”

Marta opened her laptop. The screen was black. Her battery was dead. “Does anyone have a charger?” she asked. Someone passed one across the table. She also needed five minutes to charge the phone – she had left her cable at the car park.

When she was finally ready, her manager looked at her. “Marta, can you give an update on the Oslo contract?” Marta nodded. She took a breath. Update everybody on where you are, she told herself. She knew this phrase. She had practised it.

“We received their comments last week,” she began. “There is one issue we need to discuss before we can sign. It is a matter of liability – not complicated, but important. I also have a question about the payment schedule. It depends on whether they want monthly or quarterly terms.”

The room was quiet. Then her manager said: “Good. That is exactly what we needed to hear.”

The Email of the Twelfth

Piotr runs a course for business professionals. Once a month he holds a group session online. That morning, before the session started, he received a message from one of his participants.

“I am writing in reply to your email of the twelfth of February,” the message began. “I can't join today. I can't see the chat box and my audio isn't working. Could you resend the link?”

Piotr replied immediately. He sent the link and the agenda as an attachment. “No problem,” he wrote. “Just join when you can.”

During the session, a colleague asked: “Piotr, do you worry about using so many English words in Polish? Some people say it contaminates the language.” Piotr thought for a moment. “It depends,” he said. “If the word fills a real gap, I use it. If there is a good Polish word, I use that. I do not want to overload Polish with English borrowings – but I also do not want to pretend English does not exist.”

After the session, he sent a short summary. The subject line read: Following up on your email of 12 Feb. At the end he added one line: “There is one important matter I forgot to cover today. I will address it in our next session.”
