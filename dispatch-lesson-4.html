Lesson 4 – Vocabulary
🟢  Healthcare and Medical Work
rare diseases
rzadkie choroby
She works with patients who suffer from rare diseases.
Ona pracuje z pacjentami cierpiącymi na rzadkie choroby.
Transthyretin Amyloidosis (ATTR) /ˌtrænzˌθaɪ.rəˈtiː.nən ˌæm.ɪ.lɔɪˈdoʊ.sɪs/
amyloidoza transtyretynowa
In my work I deal with ATTR – a rare and serious condition.
W swojej pracy zajmuję się ATTR – rzadką i poważną chorobą.
✅ In my work I deal with ATTR – correct.
✅ At work I deal with ATTR – also correct.
❌ In work I deal with ATTR – incorrect.
divided into
podzielony na
The condition is divided into several types.
Ta choroba jest podzielona na kilka typów.
a prescription
recepta
The doctor gave the patient a prescription for the medicine.
Lekarz dał pacjentowi receptę na ten lek.
to issue a prescription
wystawić receptę
Only a doctor can issue a prescription for this drug.
Tylko lekarz może wystawić receptę na ten lek.
to qualify for the programme
zakwalifikować się do programu
Not every patient qualifies for the programme.
Nie każdy pacjent kwalifikuje się do programu.
drug programme
program lekowy
The patient was admitted to a drug programme.
Pacjent został zakwalifikowany do programu lekowego.
✅ drug programme – standard term.
✅ publicly funded drug programme – use this when you need to clarify the Polish context.
free / free of charge / complimentary /ˌkɒmplɪˈment(ə)ri/
bezpłatny / bezpłatnie / bezpłatny (w kontekście usług)
If the patient qualifies, the treatment will be free of charge.
Jeśli pacjent się zakwalifikuje, leczenie będzie bezpłatne.
✅ free – neutral, everyday use.
✅ free of charge – more formal; common in written communications.
✅ complimentary – typical in service contexts: hotels, conferences, events. Less common in medical contexts.
🔵  Meetings Language
the output / outcome of a meeting
rezultat / efekt spotkania
The main outcome of the meeting was a list of next steps.
Głównym rezultatem spotkania była lista kolejnych kroków.
✅ output – correct, but more common in project or production contexts.
✅ outcome – more natural in meeting and discussion contexts.
to give your input
wnieść swój wkład; zabrać głos
Everyone had a chance to give their input during the discussion.
Każdy miał okazję wnieść swój wkład podczas dyskusji.
to make a contribution
wnieść wkład
Each team member made a contribution to the final report.
Każdy członek zespołu wniósł wkład do końcowego raportu.
Give your input and make a contribution overlap, but they are not the same. Input tends to refer to information, opinions, or ideas you bring to a discussion. Contribution is broader and can refer to work, effort, or a share in producing something.
people only listen to their boss
Ludzie słuchają tylko swojego szefa.
In some teams, people only listen to their boss and contributions from others are ignored.
W niektórych zespołach ludzie słuchają tylko swojego szefa i wkład innych jest ignorowany.

One Thing From Today
In my work or at work – not in work. Polish says w pracy, which pulls learners towards in work, but that combination does not exist in standard English. Say in my work I deal with rare diseases, or simply at work.
One Thing From Today
Complimentary belongs in hotels and conference centres – it means something given as a gesture, a free extra. In a medical context it is the wrong register. Use free or free of charge. The treatment is free of charge is precise. The treatment is complimentary sounds like a spa.

The Programme Meeting

The Programme Meeting

Anna had been waiting for this meeting for two weeks. It was about a drug programme for patients with rare diseases – specifically, patients who had been diagnosed with Transthyretin Amyloidosis. The condition was divided into two types, and not all patients qualified for the programme.

The room had six people in it. Anna was the only one who had read the full documentation. When the meeting started, the regional director spoke for twenty minutes without stopping. People only listened to their boss. Nobody asked a question.

Then the director looked at Anna. “Do you have anything to add?” She did. She explained that if a patient qualified, the treatment would be free of charge. The doctor only needed to issue a prescription. The patient did not pay.

The director nodded. “Good input,” he said. Anna was not sure he had understood. But she had given her input, and she had made a contribution. That was enough for today. The main outcome of the meeting was a follow-up call scheduled for the following Thursday.

Moving the Monday Call

On Friday afternoon, Kasia received a message from her Belgian colleague, Thomas. He needed to postpone their Monday meeting to another date. He was travelling and his schedule had changed.

“Could we move our meeting to Wednesday?” he wrote. “I'm sorry for the short notice.”

Kasia checked her calendar. Wednesday was difficult. She had another series of meetings in the morning, and a hospital visit in the afternoon. “I'm afraid I can't do Wednesday,” she replied. “Could we reschedule for Thursday instead? I think I can make it at two o’clock.”

Thomas agreed. He sent a new calendar invitation. At the top of the agenda he wrote: Key topic – ATTR patient referral pathway. Please come prepared to give your input on the qualification criteria.

Kasia printed the document. She had worked with patients on this drug programme for two years. She knew that when patients received their prescription through the programme, the medicine was free. She also knew that most of the doctors she worked with still did not fully understand how to qualify a patient for the programme. Thursday's meeting mattered.
